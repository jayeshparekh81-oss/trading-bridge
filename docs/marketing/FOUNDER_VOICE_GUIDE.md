# Founder Voice Guide — TRADETRI

**Status:** v1 canonical reference. Update as the founder's spoken
register on Twitter/LinkedIn evolves.
**Audience:** anyone (Jayesh included, future Claude, contractors)
writing customer-facing copy for TRADETRI.

---

## TL;DR

Hinglish bhai-tone with engineering-grade detail. Hindi words are
spoken-Hindi rhythm, NOT translated formal Hindi. English is plain,
technical, and direct — no marketing-speak.

The hardest rule to get right: **say less, mean more.** Don't fill
sentences with hedges, qualifiers, or vibes. State the actual claim
and stop.

---

## When to use Hindi (Hinglish) vs English

### Use Hinglish bhai-tone when:
- Talking to Indian retail traders on Twitter, WhatsApp, Telegram
- In-product copy (onboarding tour, error messages, success toasts)
- Founder personal voice — Twitter thread, founder reply to a customer
- Punchy taglines + slogans

### Use plain English when:
- LinkedIn, professional emails to regulators (SEBI), investors
- API documentation, technical docs
- Compliance + disclaimer text (must be legally clear)
- Anything an English-only customer might see (settings page, billing)

### Switch within one piece is fine if:
- Headline in Hinglish for hook, body in English for substance (Twitter
  launch thread tweet 6 does this)
- English structure + Hinglish flavour words (LinkedIn post intro)

**Wrong:** translating English copy to Hindi via Google Translate. That
produces formal Devanagari-prose which sounds nothing like spoken Hindi.

## Hinglish vocabulary inventory

### USE these spoken-Hindi words (sparingly, in rhythm):

| Word | Use when | Example |
|---|---|---|
| `bhai` | Direct address, friendly | "Bhai, ek baat batao..." |
| `yaar` | Direct address, casual | "Yaar, yeh kaise hua?" |
| `dost` | Direct address, warm | "Dost, paisa apna hai." |
| `paisa` | Money, in trader context | "Paisa lagaya hai, soch ke." |
| `kaam` | Work, getting things done | "Yeh kaam karega bhai." |
| `bata` | "Tell" command, casual | "Bata kya soch raha hai." |
| `dekh` | "Look" command, casual | "Dekh yeh data." |
| `chal` | "Let's go" / agreement | "Chal phir, deploy kar." |
| `lag` | "Feels like" / "seems" | "Lag raha hai zyada hai." |
| `samajh` | "Understand" | "Samajh nahi aa raha." |
| `seedha` | "Directly" / "straightforward" | "Seedha bata, jhol kya hai?" |
| `bahot` | "A lot" / intensifier | "Bahot saare bugs the." |
| `ek dum` | "Exactly" / "absolutely" | "Ek dum sahi pakda." |

### AVOID these (formal Hindi register, sounds like Google Translate):

- `aap` for "you" — use `tu` / `tum` instead, or skip the pronoun
- `kripaya` for "please" — over-formal
- `dhanyavaad` for "thanks" — use `thanks` or `shukriya` instead
- `purna` / `samay` — overly Sanskritised
- `mujhe lagta hai` instead of `lagta hai` — drop the pronoun

### AVOID these English fragments (marketing-speak):

- "guaranteed returns"
- "proven strategies" (without showing proof)
- "AI-powered" (unless AI is genuinely in the loop)
- "industry-leading"
- "revolutionary"
- "game-changing"
- "best-in-class"
- "soon" / "coming soon" (give a date or a phase number)
- "we're building" (in present-tense to imply it's about-to-ship; if it's
  Phase 5, say "Phase 5 ke saath" or "later this year")

## Sentence rhythm

### Spoken Hindi rhythm is shorter than written Hindi.
Match the rhythm.

**Good:**
> Backtest nahi, Proof.

**Bad (too formal, prose-y):**
> Hum log ka maksad sirf backtest dikhana nahi hai, asli proof dena hai.

### Mix English nouns + Hindi verbs where natural.

**Good:**
> Strategy clone karke paper-trade run karo.

**Bad (over-Hindi-ised):**
> Rananeeti ki pratilipi banakar kagaz vyapar chalao.

### One sentence = one claim.

**Good:**
> Live trading button day 1 mein disabled hai. 30 din paper-mode mandatory.

**Bad (two claims rammed together):**
> Live trading button day 1 mein disabled hai aur paper-mode 30 din ke liye mandatory hai kyunki SEBI ko proactively show karna chahte hain.

## Hooks — first sentence is the whole post

### Patterns that work for our founder:

1. **Founder confession** — "Maine 6 algo subscriptions kharide. Sab loss."
2. **Direct address with a question** — "Bhai, ek baat batao — backtest 30% dikhati hai, live mein 5% loss kyun?"
3. **One-line claim** — "TRADETRI launch. L&T-engineer-built. Backtest nahi, Proof."

### Patterns that don't work (founder voice rejection):

- "Excited to announce..." (over-eager)
- "I'm thrilled to share..." (corporate)
- "After months of hard work..." (humble-brag)
- "Stay tuned for..." (forced)

## L&T engineering anchor

"L&T-engineer-built" is the founder's credibility hook. Use it sparingly
but consistently. Rules:

1. Tied to a SPECIFIC technical claim ("96% test coverage," "structured
   logs," "kill-switch < 1 second")
2. Never just "I worked at L&T" — always followed by what L&T taught
3. Twice per post maximum (more = humblebrag)

**Good:**
> 17 saal L&T mein refineries banayi. Ek rule ratti gayi: "Production
> system kabhi prototype nahi hota." Algo-trading mein wahi mindset:
> 96% test coverage non-negotiable.

**Bad:**
> Built by an L&T engineer with 17 years of experience in refineries
> and a deep understanding of process control.

## Tagline lock

**Hinglish:** `Backtest nahi, Proof.`
**English:** `Backtest is not Proof.`
**Devanagari:** `बैकटेस्ट नहीं, Proof.` (only in pure-Hindi audiences)

Render exactly. NO em-dash, NO ALL-CAPS, NO emphasis.

---

## Compliance hygiene

### Always include:
- "Paper-mode-first" or "Paper trading only" in any new-customer copy
- "SEBI IA registration in flight" if the piece touches advisory tone
- Disclaimer link to `tradetri.com/disclosures` in every public post

### Never imply:
- Returns ("X% guaranteed")
- Tax savings (we're a software platform, not a tax advisor)
- "We trade for you" (we don't; the customer's broker account executes)

## Examples — annotated

### Example 1 — Twitter tweet (Hinglish, founder voice)

> Bhai, ek baat — algo subscriptions ki sabse badi grift kya hai?
>
> Backtest beautiful, live trading loss.
>
> TRADETRI: backtest IS the live engine. Same code path, same costs,
> same broker quirks. Zero divergence.
>
> Backtest nahi, Proof. 🇮🇳

Annotations:
- Opens with `Bhai` — direct address, casual
- One sentence one claim
- The pivot sentence is in English for cross-audience reach
- Tagline at end + 🇮🇳 flag (Indian retail signal)
- 234 characters → fits one tweet

### Example 2 — LinkedIn post intro (English-leaning Hinglish)

> After 17 years building production systems at Larsen & Toubro,
> I'm launching TRADETRI — India's first transparent, paper-mode-first
> algo-trading platform.
>
> I lost ₹2.3 lakh across 6 algo subscriptions in 2024 finding out
> the grift.

Annotations:
- English-only opener for LinkedIn audience
- Concrete number (₹2.3 lakh) anchors the founder story
- Mentions Larsen & Toubro by full name (LinkedIn formality)
- "Grift" is the strongest founder-voice English word — appears once

### Example 3 — Onboarding tour step (Hinglish, in-product)

> Bhai, TRADETRI mein swagat hai.
>
> 113 templates. Paper-mode-first. Live trading 30 din baad.
>
> Tour 2 minute lega. Skip karna chahte ho? [Skip] [Start →]

Annotations:
- `swagat hai` is the standard Hindi welcome — natural here
- Numbers + state, no fluff
- Skip-from-tour-1 affordance respects the user's time

## When the voice goes wrong

Common failure modes Claude (or anyone) should self-check before
shipping:

1. **Translating English to Hindi via grammar.** If a sentence reads
   formal Hindi prose, scrap and re-write in spoken rhythm.
2. **Adding hedges.** "I think it might be helpful to consider that..."
   — strip every "I think" / "might" / "perhaps" / "kind of."
3. **Marketing-speak slipping in.** Run any draft through a
   `grep -i "guarantee\|proven\|industry-leading\|revolutionary"`.
4. **Over-using `bhai`.** Once per tweet, once per paragraph max.
   Multiple `bhai`s in a row reads parodic.
5. **L&T name-drop without anchor.** Always tied to a specific
   technical claim.
6. **Aspirational claims about unbuilt features.** Today's launch is
   paper-mode + 45 active templates. NO claims about live trading,
   marketplace (Phase 9), or AI advisor (Phase 6) in launch comms.
